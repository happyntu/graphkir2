Responses to review comments: 
 
Reviewer: 1 
Comments to the Author 
The manuscript by Lin et al. introduces Graph-KIR, a novel graph-based computational tool 
for KIR gene copy number estimation and high-resolution allele calling from short-read 
WGS data. The tool's strengths are its innovative per-sample graph-based methodology 
and strong performance in gene-level identification. However, claims of high-resolution 
allele calling accuracy are not sufficiently benchmarked against the current state-of-the-art. 
Furthermore, the evaluation on real-world data relies on filtering that limits claims of 
practical utility. The work requires major revisions to substantiate its claimed advantages 
before it can be recommended for publication. 
 
--- MAJOR COMMENTS --- 
 
1. The manuscript requires a direct benchmark against the state-of-the-art Geny tool. 
 
  - I recognize the Graph-KIR preprint was originally posted in 2023, before the publication 
of Geny (Zhou et al., 2024). However, Geny now represents the benchmark for accuracy, 
particularly at the functionally significant 3-digit resolution: 
    * Geny: 90.9% precision, 95.3% recall (40 HPRC samples) 
    * Graph-KIR: 76.4% accuracy (44 HPRC samples) 
 
  - The Geny authors stated they tried Graph-KIR but "were unable to get satisfactory 
results". While this claim is weak as the Geny authors did not provide additional evidence, it 
does need to be taken into account. Due to the published accuracy values of Geny, I cannot 
support the authors claims of that their tool is more accurate than the competition (eg "the 
results showcase that Graph-KIR remains superior to the alternative regarding of accuracy 
at all resolutions while maintaining a low number of false negatives"). Ultimately, the lack of 
reciprocal benchmarking by either group creates a significant gap in comparative evaluation 
and needs to be addressed. 
Response: 
We thank the reviewer for this essential comment. We acknowledge that Geny (Zhou et al., 
2024) has established a high standard for KIR typing. In this revision, we have performed a 
comprehensive, reciprocal benchmark against Geny using both simulated data and the full 
HPRC dataset. 
To address the discrepancies noted by the reviewer, we have implemented the 
following improvements and clarifications: 
===== PAGE 2 =====
(1) Standardization of Metrics: We have corrected the terminology in our manuscript. 
The metric previously labeled as "Accuracy" was, in fact, Recall. We now provide a 
full suite of metrics—Recall, Precision, and F1-score—for all tools to ensure a 
transparent comparison. Notably, our analysis of the Geny source code revealed 
that its internal recall calculation excludes "mis-paired" alleles from the 
denominator, which can lead to an overestimation of performance. To ensure a 
rigorous benchmark, we have recalculated Geny’s performance using the standard 
definitions above for all comparisons in this study. 
(2) Benchmark on HPRC Data (44 Samples): We conducted the evaluation based on 
the 44 HPRC samples, larger than the 40 samples used in the Geny publication. 
The results on the simulated data (Tables 1 and 3) and the real data (Tables 2 and 
4) demonstrate a nuanced performance landscape: 
A. Copy Number Estimation: Graph-KIR consistently outperforms existing 
methods, providing the robust foundation necessary for accurate allele calling. 
(Table 1 and Table 2) 
B. 7-digit Resolution: Graph-KIR remains superior to other tools, including 
Geny, in resolving non-coding variations. (Table 3 and Table 4) 
C. Functional Typing (3-digit): Graph-KIR achieves higher Recall, whereas 
Geny demonstrates higher Precision. (Table 3 and Table 4) 
(3) To address the "unsatisfactory results" mentioned by the Geny authors, we 
identified that a primary source of confusion was the inclusion of non-coding 
variants in functional (3-digit) calls. In this revision, we emphasize Graph-KIR’s 
'exon-only' configuration. This option restricts the variant set to exonic regions, 
ensuring that our 3-digit calls align strictly with the functional nomenclature used by 
Geny and other tools. 
(4) We have tempered our language to reflect these findings. Rather than claiming 
absolute superiority in every metric, we now specify that Graph-KIR provides the 
highest resolution (7-digit) accuracy and the most robust copy number estimation 
currently available, while remaining competitive at functional resolutions. 
 
2. Performance evaluation on HPRC data overstates the tool's practical utility due to the 
filtering of complex alleles. 
 
  - The authors report their highest accuracy only after filtering out novel alleles, fusion 
events, or other complex variations. A user analyzing a new sample will not know a priori if 
such complexities exist. Reporting accuracy on a "cleaned" dataset does not reflect the 
tool's performance in a true discovery setting. The authors should present results on the 
complete, unfiltered HPRC dataset and discuss the tool's performance and limitations with 
===== PAGE 3 =====
these challenging features, providing specific examples of what happens in these cases. 
Response: 
We thank the reviewer for this critical perspective on "discovery setting" performance. We 
agree that reporting only filtered results does not fully represent the tool's utility in a real-
world clinical or research context where the underlying genomic complexity is unknown. 
In the revised manuscript, we have made the following changes: 
(1) Unfiltered Benchmarking: We now present the performance of Graph-KIR, PING, 
and Geny on the complete, unfiltered HPRC dataset (44 samples) in Table 4. This 
provides a baseline for the tool’s performance in a true discovery setting, including 
all novel alleles and complex fusion events. 
(2) We have repositioned the "filtered" results as a secondary analysis (Supplementary 
Table S2). This serves as a diagnostic evaluation to quantify exactly how much 
specific features, such as gene fusions or novel polymorphisms, impact calling 
accuracy for each tool. 
(3) We emphasize that even in the presence of novel variations, Graph-KIR’s graph-
based alignment maintains higher 7-digit resolution accuracy than alternative 
methods. We anticipate that this advantage will become increasingly critical as the 
IPD-KIR database expands to incorporate a broader catalog of novel alleles in the 
near future. 
 
3. The simulation methodology may not adequately capture the complexity of the KIR locus. 
 
  - The current simulation generates reads directly from KIR allele sequences, excluding 
inter-gene sequences. This simplifies the read mapping challenge by removing homologous 
sequences and segmental duplications. The large discrepancy in simulated vs real data 
accuracy further supports the notion that the simulations are not sufficiently representative 
(91% vs 77.8%). A more robust validation would simulate reads from the entire ~150kb KIR 
genomic locus, and swap out alleles from the database within the gene locations. 
Response: 
We appreciate the reviewer’s constructive critique regarding our simulation strategy. We 
acknowledge that simulating reads directly from cDNA or isolated gene sequences can 
underestimate the challenges posed by the high homology and repetitive nature of the 
intergenic regions within the ~150kb KIR cluster. Nevertheless, these simulations still 
demonstrate clear performance differences between the various methods (Tables 1 and 
Table 3).  
 To test whether intergenic reads contribute to the discrepancy between simulated 
(91.79% F1-score) and real-world (68.67% F1-score) performance, we conducted an 
experiment where we filtered out reads mapping to intergenic regions (based on GRCh38 
===== PAGE 4 =====
coordinates) in the 44 HPRC samples. We found that this filtering process had no 
significant impact on the allele-calling performance (data not shown). This suggest that 
while intergenic homology is a known challenge for mapping, the performance "gap" is 
more likely driven by other complex structural variations and novel polymorphisms present 
in the HPRC assemblies rather than intergenic noise alone.  
We have now explicitly detailed these "failure modes" in Supplementary Table S2. Both 
Graph-KIR and Geny are similarly impacted by features such as: gene fusion events, large 
segmental duplications, or novel alleles. 
We also considered using the simulation framework proposed by Zhou et al. (Geny). 
However, we observed that their framework generates and evaluates reads for each 
haploid assembly separately. In contrast, real-world WGS data is derived from diploid 
samples, where reads from two different haplotypes must be de-convoluted simultaneously. 
We believe our simulation, while simplified in terms of intergenic sequence, more accurately 
captures the diploid de-convolution challenge, which is a primary hurdle in KIR typing. 
 
4. The ground truth evaluation methodology is unclear 
 
   - The authors state that the HPRC ground truth evaluation was taken from Hung et. al. 
2024 "Genetic complexity of killer-cell immunoglobulin-like receptor genes in human 
pangenome assemblies" - it is not immediately clear that this is the SKIRT paper. The 
authors should state clearly whether SKIRT was used, as is available on github, for the 
ground truth evaluation. 
   - The methods for HPRC ground truth calling in the Graph-KIR github do not include the 
`kelvin_kir_v1_0.csv` file that contains the HPRC ground truth allele calls necessary to 
reproduce the results. I could not find that file in any of the supplemental code, files or 
zenodo deposits from Hung et. al. The authors should ensure their ground truth 
methodology is reproducible, either by including all files or directly users how to use SKIRT 
to reproduce them 
Response: 
We thank the reviewer for pointing out the need for greater clarity regarding our ground 
truth data. We have made the following updates to the manuscript and our public repository 
to ensure full transparency and reproducibility: 
1. We have revised the section 2.8 to state clearly that the HPRC ground truth 
annotations, the Supplemental Table S2 of Hung et al., were generated using 
SKIRT (Hung et al., 2024).  
2. We apologize for the missing file in our previous repository. We have now uploaded 
the comprehensive ground truth file, hprc_summary_v1_2_e.tsv, to the Graph-KIR 
GitHub repository. This file contains the finalized HPRC ground truth allele calls 
===== PAGE 5 =====
utilized in our evaluation, corresponding to Supplementary Table S2 from Hung et 
al. (2024). Furthermore, we have provided the scripts for calculating Recall, 
Precision, and F1-score across all resolutions within our GitHub repository. 
3. We selected SKIRT as our ground truth framework based on its rigorous adherence 
to IPD-KIR nomenclature and its high performance relative to other tools: 
A. SKIRT utilizes a sequence-based hierarchical classification. The first three 
digits are defined by protein sequence identity, digits four and five distinguish 
synonymous variants within the CDS, and the final two digits characterize 
non-coding variations. 
B. In our validation of the 47 HPRC genome assemblies, SKIRT demonstrated: 
99.7% concordance with Immuannot, while successfully identifying three gene 
fusion events that Immuannot failed to detect. BAKIR showed significantly 
lower concordance with both SKIRT (94.2%) and Immuannot (93.9%) and 
was only evaluated on 26 of the 47 HPRC samples. 
4. By providing the raw annotation files and clearly defining the hierarchical logic of 
the SKIRT pipeline, we ensure that our evaluation methodology is both technically 
sound and fully reproducible by the community.

Reviewer: 2 
Comments to the Author 
The manuscript introduces Graph-KIR, a graph-based method for KIR copy number 
estimation and allele typing from short-read sequencing data. The KIR locus is one of the 
most challenging regions of the human genome due to its high polymorphism, structural 
variation, and paralogous gene families, and the development of tools tailored to this 
complexity is both timely and important. The paper is generally well-written and provides a 
clear description of the pipeline, as well as comparisons to existing approaches. However, I 
have some concerns outlined below: 
 
Major: 
- Merging highly similar genes (KIR2DL1/2DS1, KIR2DL5A/5B) reduces false discovery but 
risks masking biologically relevant distinctions. 
Response: 
By utilizing a merged MSA for paralogous pairs like KIR2DL5A/B, we allow the graph-based 
model to evaluate all potential variations across both loci simultaneously. This prevents the 
loss of reads that might otherwise be discarded due to mapping ambiguity between nearly 
identical references. 
 Following the alignment phase, Graph-KIR performs allele calling for each gene 
independently. The tool utilizes locus-specific diagnostic variations within the graph to 
distinguish between the genes. Consequently, the final output clearly reports separate allele 
types for KIR2DL5A and KIR2DL5B (or KIR2DL1 and KIR2DS1). 
 This approach ensures that we reduce false negatives caused by mapping interference 
while fully preserving the biologically relevant distinctions between separate loci. 
 
- The model assumes uniform sequencing depth and discards multi-mapped reads, which 
could bias CN estimates in low-complexity regions.   
Response: 
We thank the reviewer for this technical critique. We would like to clarify our strategy for 
handling read depth and multi-mapping: 
(1) We intentionally discard multi-mapped reads because their inclusion often leads to 
a significant over-estimation of local depth, introducing unpredictable noise that 
biases copy number (CN) estimation. By focusing on uniquely assigned reads 
within the graph, we ensure a more reliable baseline. 
(2) We acknowledge that removing these reads may lead to a slight under-estimation 
of depth for certain high-homology genes. To address this, Graph-KIR does not rely 
===== PAGE 7 =====
on a fixed depth value. Instead, we model the depth variation using a normal 
distribution based on estimated mean and standard deviation. This distribution-
based approach provides the necessary tolerance for potential under-estimation, 
allowing for robust CN calling. 
(3) Our results across diverse datasets confirm that this statistical tolerance effectively 
compensates for the removal of multi-mapped reads, as evidenced by our high 
accuracy in CN estimation compared to ground truth. 
 
- The authors use hg19 (hs37d5) instead of hg38, citing complications with hg38 scaffolds. 
This choice is outdated and may limit adoption in future studies... Most recent studies and 
reference resources have moved to hg38 or T2T-CHM13; reliance on hg19 may hinder 
reproducibility and comparability. 
Response: 
We thank the reviewer for this important observation regarding the reference genome 
version. We acknowledge that hg38 and T2T-CHM13 are the current standards for most 
genomic analyses. Our initial use of hg19 (hs37d5) was a deliberate choice due to the 
extreme complexity of the KIR region; hg38 contains numerous KIR-related alternative 
scaffolds that require cautious handling to avoid multi-mapping artifacts and biased read 
depth. 
However, we would like to clarify that Graph-KIR is fully compatible with hg38. To 
address the reviewer’s concern, we performed additional testing using a scaffold-free 
version of hg38 as the reference for retrieving KIR-related reads. Our results demonstrate: 
(a) The performance of Graph-KIR when using hg38 is consistent with the results 
obtained using hg19. This indicates that the underlying graph-based approach is 
robust to the reference version used for initial read recruitment. 
(b) The users of Graph-KIR can speficy the reference version (hg19 or hg38) used. 
Graph-KIR then adjusts its retrieval coordinates accordingly. 
We have added a note in the section 2.3 of the revised manuscript to explicitly state 
that both hg19 and hg38 are supported and yield comparable performance. 
 
- Simulated data are fixed at 30x, but real samples vary. A systematic downsampling 
experiment (e.g., 5x–30x) would be important to establish the tool’s performance at varying 
depths. 
Response: 
We thank the reviewer for highlighting the importance of depth-dependency analysis. While 
systematic downsampling of simulated data can provide a theoretical baseline, we believe 
that evaluating performance across real-world sequencing depth variation provides a more 
rigorous and clinically relevant assessment of the tool's robustness. 
===== PAGE 8 =====
To address this, we utilized the inherent depth variability in the 44 HPRC samples, 
which ranges from 17x to 70x (as detailed in Supplementary Table S1). We categorized 
these samples into three cohorts (<30x, 30–40x, and >40x) to evaluate performance across 
functional and genomic resolutions: 
Our results in Supplementary Table S3 indicate that a reduction in sequencing depth to 
below 30x does not significantly compromise calling accuracy. This suggests that Graph-
KIR’s graph-based recruitment is highly efficient even with limited data. 
Interestingly, we observed slightly lower performance in the highest-depth group 
(>40x). Our investigation revealed that this group consists primarily of early-released HPRC 
samples. These samples might be characterized by lower base-calling quality. Similar trend 
is observed on the results of Geny. Therefore, we consider that sequence quality, rather 
than depth, is the primary driver of performance variance in these cases. 
 
Minor: 
- Although Graph-KIR improves short-read analysis of KIR, short reads are inherently 
limited in resolving paralogous genes and complex haplotypes compared to long-read or 
graph-based approaches. The authors should briefly acknowledge this limitation in the 
discussion. 
Response: 
We agree that short-read sequencing has physical limitations in resolving the most complex 
KIR structural variations. We have added a concise acknowledgment of this in the 
Discussion section, noting that while long-reads offer superior phasing, Graph-KIR remains 
a critical tool for high-resolution analysis of existing large-scale short-read datasets. 
 
- The sentence “When evaluated with both simulated and real data, the superior 
performance of Graph-KIR over other existing KIR tools” is incomplete and should be 
revised for grammatical clarity. 
Response: 
Thank you for the correction. We fixed the sentence in the revised manuscript. 
Furthermore, we have updated the manuscript to further emphasize the primary 
advantages of Graph-KIR. Specifically, we highlight its superior performance in copy 
number estimation and 7-digit allele calling. While many existing tools are optimized for 3-
digit functional typing, Graph-KIR’s graph-based approach allows for a more granular 
characterization of the KIR region, providing high-accuracy results at the full genomic 
sequence level (7-digit) and robust copy number profiles across both simulated and real 
datasets. 
 
- “full-resolution (7-digit)” in the abstract could confuse readers unfamiliar with KIR/HLA

nomenclature. A short clarifier would help. 
Response: 
We appreciate the reviewer’s suggestion to clarify the nomenclature for a broader 
audience. We recognize that while "7-digit" is a standard term within the KIR and HLA
